Social Influence in Public and Private Behaviors in Social Advertising: A Large-Scale Randomized Controlled Trial (Job Market Paper)

 
The degree and process of social influence is likely to differ between public and private behaviors and such difference remains unexamined. By randomly manipulating the number of social cues (i.e. friends’ endorsements of ads) displayed on ads for more than 37 million WeChat users, I identify and compare social influence in consumers' public (i.e. liking) and private (i.e. clicking) responses to social ads. I find that the degree of social influence in liking up to more than double the influence in clicking, while both of them were susceptible to a significant degree of influence. Both of the normative and informational influence drive users' responses to social ads and are stronger in liking than in clicking. This work presents some of the first large-scale experimental evidence identifying the value of social influence for social ads effectiveness and investigating the degree and the process of social influence in different behaviors.

Social Influence Across Products: A Large-Scale Randomized Experiment in Social Advertising  (with Sinan Aral, Jeffrey (Yu) Hu and Erik Brynjolfsson)

 
Spending on social advertising is increasingly dramatically. Unfortunately, large-scale experimental evidence of the lift from social ads almost always focuses on a single product at a time. As a result, we know little about how social influence and the effectiveness of social advertising vary across product categories. We, therefore conducted a randomized field experiment measuring social influence and social ad effectiveness across 71 products in 25 product categories among a random sample of more than 37 million users of WeChat Moments Ads. We analyzed this experiment to causally identify the heterogeneous effects of social influence and social advertising across different products and product categories and to investigate how product characteristics impact social advertising effectiveness. We found some product categories, like food, clothes, and cars, experienced significantly stronger social advertising effectiveness than other categories like financial services and electrical appliances. More generally, we found that status goods, which rely on normative social influence, display strong social advertising effectiveness, but the social ads for experience goods, which rely on informational social influence, do not perform any better or worse than for search goods. Understanding the heterogeneous effects of social influence across different products will help marketers differentiate their social advertising strategies and enable platform managers to optimally price social ads.
